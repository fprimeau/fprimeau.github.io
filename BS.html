1. ANY VISION IS BETTER THAN NO VISION

Without a vision we are doomed to wanter like drunken sailors. We want n not root n.

QUANTITATIVE PROBLEM SOLVING FIRST, VOCABULARY AND CONCEPTS BY OSMOSIS

We envision a program that emphasizes quantitative problem solving: Focus on creating graduates adept at quantitative problem solving by immersing them in an intensive quantitative curriculum.

Prepare for Lifelong Learning: Equip students with the skills needed for continual learning, acknowledging the rapidly evolving nature of technical knowledge.

Focus on fundamentals prioritize fundamental mathematical training to provide a strong foundation for abstract thinking applicable across various context. The half-life of technical knowledge seems to be getting shorter all the time.

High-paying jobs that make use of mathematics are expected to grow at a rate of 30 percent over the next decade. https://www.bls.gov/ooh/math/home.htm

We want problem solvers, not button pushers

3. NEW BS CORE

3.1. EARTHSS X1 Math Functions and Computers (3u)

  • This course provides a very gentle introduction to using a computer to think about mathematical modeling using functions
  • It also reviews the basic functions needed to succeed in MATH 2A
  • Learn to read in and write data to a file
  • Learn to plot data and functions
  • Review linear and quadratic functions
  • Solving systems of two linear equations using pencil and paper (e.g. change of temperature from deg F to deg C and vice versa)
  • Solving systems of two equations one linear and one quadratic using pencil and paper (acid base problems could provide examples)
  • Review of exponentials (plotting pressure as a function of height in the atmosphere could provide a good example)
  • Review of logarithms ([H3O-] concentration and pH could provide a good example)
  • Review of function composition f(g(x)) and inverse functions
  • Review of transformations in the plane, re-scaling in x and or/y, translation in x and/or y, rotation, reflection
  • Review of trigonometric functions (coordinates on the surface of the Earth could provide good examples)
  • Students review function composition and function inverses (lots of practice using exponentials and logarithms)
  • Students review polynomial functions ( acid-base systems such as CO2 in sea water provide good examples )
  • Review of rational function (the slope of a curve could provide good examples, Michalis Menton growth limitation could provide more examples)
  • Find the zeros of a polynomial graphically on a computer (i.e., plot and zoom!)
  • Students review vector addition, the dot product and the cross product (good practice problems could be to find the distance between two points on the surface of a sphere, will also help students appreciate the "magic" under the hood of gis software!)
  • Plot the data using scatter plots, line plots, histograms, bar-charts
  • Drill in that plots must have labels with appropriate units
  • Compute the average or sample mean of data
  • Compute the variance of a bunch of data
  • Compute the standard deviation of a bunch of data

3.2. EARTHSS X2 Dimensions, Units, Orders of Magnitude (2u) (Should be offerred to BA and BS students and would be the place to reach all our freshmen students)

  • Learn about dimensional and dimensionless quantities
  • Learn that some quantities have units but no dimensions (e.g., degrees and radians percents permills )
  • Learn that the arguments of exponents, logarithms, and trig functions have to be dimensionless
  • Learn that arguments of polynomials can have units, but that the coefficients must have compatible units
  • Learn that plots must be labeled so as to indicate the units
  • Practice with scientific notation
  • Lots of practice doing back-of-the-envelope, orders-of-magnitude, Fermi problems, including lots of rate problems
  • Gentle introduction to getting students to start thinking about the differences between stocks and flows and also about the order of magnitude and units of various stocks and flows relevant to Earth System Science Good examples from the flux of solar energy and from the hydrological cycle.

3.3. EARTHSS X3 Introduction to GIS (4u) This could be open to BA students

  • How to use the software
  • Discrete vs Continuous Spatial Data (vector vs raster files)
  • gentle introduction to fields relevant to Earth System Science
  • More practice with data wrangling, read in data from files in various formats
  • …

3.4. EARTHSS X4 Conservation Principles on a Computer (Pre-requisite EARTHSS X3 or some other computer programming course) (4u)

  • Introduction to stocks and flows
  • Introduction to steady state box models (lots of ESS applications, review of solving linear systems)
  • Concept of residence time
  • Simulation of transient box models (lots of ESS applications, re-enforce concepts from Math 2A )
  • Emphasis on "systems" thinking

3.5. EARTHSS X5 Earth System Sciencd Data Analysis (Pre-requisite MATH 3A, MATH 2E, and EARTHSS X4) (4u)

  • Assumes that the students have a certain mathematical maturity
  • Estimate the mean and variance of a sample population and assign error bars to these quantitites
  • Find a line of best fit by minimizing the residual sum of squares
  • Find the polynomial of best fit by minimizing the residual sum of squares
  • Re-enforces the idea that the slope = 0 at the minimum
  • Lots of applications of completing the square to decompose the variance into the part capture by the model and the residual part (apply and reinforce the concepts learned in EARTHSS X1 and X3)
  • Make histograms of residuals to get an intuitive understanding of the normal distribution and the standard deviation
  • Use random number generators to make synthetic data sets to practice data fitting and hone intuition about noisy systems ( introduction to stochastic simulations )
  • Learn how to fit and interpret linear regression models to multi-dimensional data
  • Learn about phenomenology of large-scale atmospheric variability (all those O's ENSO, PDO, NAO, etc.) using reanalysis products and CMIP output

3.6. EARTHSS X6 Applied Machine Learning (Pre-requisite EARTHSS X5) (4u)

  • Applications of clustering algorithms
  • Applications of neural networks.
  • Learn about ESS phenomenology using remote sensing products and reanalysis products

3.7. EARTHSS X7 Atmospheric Physics (co-requisite ESS 142) (4u,4u)

  • ideal gas law
  • green house gases
  • radiative convective equilibrium
  • the greenhouse effect
  • build a simple radiative-convective equilibrium model for an atmospheric column

3.8. EARTHSS X14 Remote Sensing (Pre-requisite EARTHSS X5) (4u)

  • Principles of remote sensing with applications to Earth System Science

3.9. EARTHSS X9,10,11 Climate Dynamics (4u,4u,4u)

  • Atmospheric Boundary Layer and Surface Ocean Mixed layer
  • Large scale circulation of the atmosphere and ocean
  • Dominant dynamical balances (hydrostatic, geostrophic, thermal wind balances)
  • Hydrological cycles
  • …

3.10. EARTHSS X12 Aquatic Chemistry (4u)

  • Acid base and redox requations in natural systems
  • Learn to set up the mathematical problem
  • Learn to find the solution
  • Learn to interpret the solution

3.11. EARTHSS X13 Global Biogeochemical Cycles (pre-requisite EARTHSS X4 EARTHSS 142, EARTHSS X12)

  • More box modeling
  • The global Carbon cycle, the Nitrogen cycle, the Phosphorus cycle, the Sulfur cycle
  • This is the grand synthesis course for biogeochemcial cycles.

3.12. EARTHSS X20, X21W

  • a data analysis and/or simulation project with presentation and term paper
